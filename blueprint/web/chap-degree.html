3 The degree homomorphism

Each double coset splits into finitely many left cosets, and counting them defines a ring homomorphism from the Hecke ring to \(\mathbb {Z}\) that records the size of each operator.

  • HeckeRing.HeckeCoset_deg

Let \((G,H,\Delta )\) be a Hecke pair and let \(D=HgH\) be a double coset. Writing \(D=\bigsqcup _i Hg_i\) for its decomposition into finitely many left cosets, the degree of \(D\) is

\[ \deg D \; =\; [\, H : H\cap gHg^{-1}\, ], \]

the number of left cosets \(Hg_i\) comprising \(D\).

  • HeckeRing.smulOrbit_card

Fix a double coset \(D=HgH\) with left-coset representatives \(g_i\), and let \(H\beta \) be any left coset with \(\beta \in \Delta \). Then the orbit

\[ \{ \, Hg_i\beta : i\, \} \]

has cardinality equal to \(\deg D\).

Proof ▶

The map sending the \(i\)-th representative to the left coset \(H(\beta g_i g)\) is a surjection onto the orbit by construction, so it suffices to show it is injective. If two representatives produced the same coset, then after left-cancelling \(\beta \) the corresponding cosets \(Hg_i g\) and \(Hg_j g\) would meet, hence coincide; this contradicts the fact that distinct representatives index distinct left cosets in the decomposition of \(D\). Thus the orbit has exactly as many elements as there are left cosets in \(D\), namely \(\deg D\) [Sh, §3.1].

  • HeckeRing.deg

The degree homomorphism is the ring homomorphism

\[ \deg : \mathbb {T} \longrightarrow \mathbb {Z} \]

determined by sending each basis element \([D]\) to \(\deg D\) and extending \(\mathbb {Z}\)-linearly; that is, \(\deg \bigl(\sum _D a_D\, [D]\bigr)=\sum _D a_D\, \deg D\). It sends the identity to \(1\), is additive, and is multiplicative [Sh, §3.1, Prop. 3.3].

Lean declarations

  • HeckeRing.deg_mul

For all \(f,g\) in the Hecke ring,

\[ \deg (f\cdot g)=\deg (f)\, \deg (g). \]
Proof ▶

Since \(\deg \) is additive, it suffices to verify multiplicativity on a pair of basis elements \([D_1]\) and \([D_2]\). Expanding the product in the Hecke ring via the structure constants gives \([D_1]\cdot [D_2]=\sum _d \mu (D_1,D_2;d)\, [d]\), so by definition

\[ \deg \bigl([D_1]\cdot [D_2]\bigr)=\sum _d \mu (D_1,D_2;d)\, \deg d. \]

The right-hand side counts, with multiplicity, the left cosets occurring in the product double coset. Grouping these by the right action of \(D_2\) on the left cosets of \(D_1\) and applying the orbit-size identity, each orbit contributes exactly \(\deg D_2\) cosets, and there are \(\deg D_1\) orbits; hence the total is \(\deg D_1\, \deg D_2\) [Sh, §3.1].
